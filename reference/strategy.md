# STRATEGY - product strategy

Frameworks for deciding where to play and how to win. Strategy is a set of choices, not a list of
goals; every canvas must end in a defensible bet, not a description.

## product-strategy (9-section canvas)

1. Vision (the future you are creating) - 2. Target customer + the job - 3. Problem / insight -
4. Value proposition - 5. Differentiation / moat - 6. Business model (how value is captured) -
7. Go-to-market - 8. Key metrics (North Star + inputs) - 9. Risks + assumptions. Output: each
section in 1-3 lines, the central bet stated explicitly, and the one assumption that most
endangers it.

## startup-canvas

Product strategy + business model on one page for early ventures: problem, customer, value prop,
solution, channels, revenue, cost, key metrics, unfair advantage. Use when there is no product
yet and you need strategy and economics together.

## product-vision

A vivid, time-bound picture of the world once you have won. Format options: vision statement
(for <customer> who <need>, <product> is a <category> that <benefit>; unlike <alt>, it <diff>),
or a "future press release" (Amazon working-backwards). Output: one statement + the change in the
customer's life it implies.

## value-proposition (6-part JTBD)

1. Customer job (functional, emotional, social) - 2. Pains - 3. Gains - 4. Pain relievers -
5. Gain creators - 6. Products/features. Fit = relievers/creators map to the highest-severity
pains/gains. Output: the canvas + a one-sentence value prop and the pain it most relieves.

## lean-canvas (9 blocks, Maurya)

Problem | Customer Segments | Unique Value Proposition | Solution | Channels | Revenue Streams |
Cost Structure | Key Metrics | Unfair Advantage. Built for new/uncertain ventures (replaces BMC's
Key Partners/Activities/Resources with Problem/Solution/Unfair Advantage).

## business-model (Business Model Canvas, 9 blocks)

Customer Segments | Value Propositions | Channels | Customer Relationships | Revenue Streams |
Key Resources | Key Activities | Key Partnerships | Cost Structure. Use for an established
business reasoning about how the whole model fits.

## monetization-strategy

Generate 3-5 ways to capture value: subscription, usage-based, freemium, transactional, seat,
tiered, marketplace take-rate, ads. For each: what you charge for, why it fits the value metric,
and the cheapest experiment to validate willingness to pay. Recommend one.

## pricing-strategy

- **Model:** value metric (the unit you charge by - seats, events, GB), tiers, packaging.
- **Level:** willingness-to-pay (Van Westendorp price-sensitivity, or feature-value surveys),
  competitor anchoring, value-based ceiling.
- **Elasticity:** how demand moves with price; where the margin-maximizing point sits.
- **Output:** a recommended model + price points + the WTP evidence and the test to confirm.

## Analysis frameworks (use to inform, not to fill space)

- **SWOT** - Strengths/Weaknesses (internal), Opportunities/Threats (external). End with the 1-2
  strategic implications, not a 4-quadrant list.
- **PESTLE** - Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Legal, Environmental macro forces.
  Keep only forces that change a decision.
- **Porter's Five Forces** - rivalry, new entrants, supplier power, buyer power, substitutes.
  Output: where the industry's profit pressure concentrates and what it means for the bet.
- **Ansoff matrix** - market penetration / product development / market development /
  diversification. Use to classify a growth move and surface its risk profile.

## Critic completeness anchors

Every canvas ends in an explicit bet and a named riskiest assumption; analysis frameworks end in
a decision implication, never a bare quadrant; pricing recommends a model with WTP evidence (not
invented numbers); value-prop relievers actually map to the named pains.
